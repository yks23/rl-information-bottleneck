Dataset geometry

RL sharpens a broad distribution into problem seeds.

Train on source prompt i, evaluate target prompt j, and the directed transfer matrix becomes a map of dataset distance. Strong diagonal dominance means most information remains prompt-local; sparse off-diagonal edges reveal reusable skills and curriculum anchors.

Design implication

To make LLM math RL more AlphaZero-like, renew the frontier.

Allocate rollouts to high-information prompts, generate new variants, use dense or process feedback where it is aligned, and use transfer geometry to avoid redundant data.

A useful way to say it

Why does math RL still improve today?

Because the post-training teams at frontier labs are, collectively, acting like the missing AlphaZero opponent. Human researchers, annotators, eval builders, data miners, contest problems, synthetic generators, and repeated model releases create a costly artificial self-play loop: every time the model gets stronger, the surrounding organization pushes a harder frontier back at it.

This works, but it is an expensive simulation of a renewable environment. As models improve, the frontier requires more tasks, more evaluators, and more precisely targeted failures than humans alone can supply. The natural endpoint is not just better human curation; it is AI systems that generate, verify, and organize their own next frontier.
